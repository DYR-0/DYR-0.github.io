Teaching Philosophy Statement

As an educator in disaster resilience, urban informatics, and data-driven civil infrastructure systems, my teaching philosophy centers on three core commitments: (1) human-centered and inclusive learning, (2) active and authentic engagement, and (3) metacognitive growth that empowers students as independent thinkers. I believe meaningful learning occurs when students connect technical knowledge with real-world problems that matter to their communities. My goal is to guide students not only in mastering computational tools, but also in understanding how data, modeling, and technology intersect with equity, ethics, and public well-being.

1. Inclusive, Strength-Based Pedagogy

Grounded in Universal Design for Learning (UDL), I design learning experiences that acknowledge the diversity of learners' strengths, backgrounds, and needs. Students are given multiple ways to engage—through hands-on analysis, discussion, writing, visualization, and computational modeling. I aim to reduce unnecessary barriers so all learners can access core ideas. For example, in a network analysis activity, beginners may use a scaffolded notebook while advanced students extend the model with their own parameters. This structure helps every student remain challenged and supported.

2. Active Learning and Real-World Application

My courses integrate frequent opportunities for students to apply theory to real datasets, particularly those related to hazards, infrastructure, and community resilience. Students learn through doing: conducting accessibility analysis with OSMnx, modeling disaster impacts, evaluating social vulnerability, or building small-scale knowledge graphs. These activities build technical fluency while helping students see the societal implications of their work. I design assignments to be open-ended, encouraging curiosity, creativity, and interdisciplinary reasoning.

3. Facilitating Critical Thinking About Equity and Ethics

Engineering and data science decisions carry deep ethical consequences. I help students develop a systems mindset that considers not only computational accuracy but also equity impacts, limitations of data, and the complexities of lived experience. Discussions, case studies, and reflective writing invite students to interrogate assumptions embedded in infrastructure models and hazard metrics. My goal is to support students in becoming not only competent engineers and analysts but also thoughtful contributors to society.

4. Supporting Metacognition and Growth

I view my role as a guide who helps students develop awareness of how they learn. Through structured reflection, iterative feedback, and opportunities for revision, students practice metacognitive skills essential for lifelong learning. I emphasize process over perfection, encouraging experimentation, productive struggle, and intellectual humility.

Conclusion

Ultimately, I teach to empower students to use data, technology, and critical inquiry to build safer, more equitable, and more resilient communities. My pedagogy blends technical rigor with human-centered values, cultivating an environment where all learners can grow, contribute, and thrive.

Innovative Assignment

Mapping Disaster Accessibility & Equity through Network Analysis

Overview

This assignment enables students to analyze how road closures during disasters affect community accessibility and equity. Using Python, OSMnx, and GIS datasets, students will identify isolated communities and interpret the social equity implications of their findings.

Learning Outcomes

Students will be able to:

  • Construct and analyze a road network graph using OSMnx
  • Compute travel-time accessibility to essential facilities (e.g., hospitals)
  • Compare baseline vs. disrupted conditions and quantify isolation
  • Interpret how accessibility changes affect vulnerable populations
  • Present findings through maps, narrative analysis, and reflection

Assignment Description

Students will complete a 5-step workflow:

Step 1: Data Preparation

Load baseline road network and disaster road closure dataset

Step 2: Baseline Analysis

Build a drivable network graph and compute shortest-path travel time to the nearest essential facility

Step 3: Disruption Modeling

Incorporate closures/disruptions and recompute accessibility

Step 4: Visualization

Create maps showing isolated tracts and quantify travel-time increases

Step 5: Analysis & Reflection

Write a 2–3 page report discussing:

  • Accessibility changes
  • Spatial patterns
  • Demographic or vulnerability implications
  • Model limitations

Submission Format

Technical Components

  • Jupyter Notebook (.ipynb)
  • Map visualizations (PNG/PDF)

Written Analysis

  • Short analytical report (PDF)
  • 2-3 pages

Why This Assignment Is Innovative

  • Uses real disaster datasets and real-world decision workflows
  • Integrates computational modeling + ethics + equity
  • Allows multiple entry points (UDL): scaffolded templates for beginners, optional extensions for advanced learners
  • Demonstrates a modern, interdisciplinary way to teach spatial analysis

Second Innovative Assignment

Building a Knowledge Graph for Infrastructure Interdependencies

Overview

This assignment challenges students to construct a knowledge graph that models infrastructure interdependencies during disasters. Students will use real disaster event data to map relationships between infrastructure systems (power, water, transportation, communication) and identify cascading failure patterns. This assignment develops systems thinking, data modeling skills, and understanding of urban resilience complexity.

Learning Outcomes

Students will be able to:

  • Design an ontology for infrastructure systems and disaster impacts
  • Extract and structure relationships from disaster reports and datasets
  • Build and query a knowledge graph using Python (NetworkX or Neo4j)
  • Identify critical infrastructure nodes and cascading failure pathways
  • Communicate complex system interdependencies through visual and written analysis

Assignment Description

Working individually or in pairs, students will complete a 4-phase project:

Phase 1: Data Collection & Analysis

Review disaster event documentation (e.g., Hurricane Maria, Texas Winter Storm 2021). Extract entities (infrastructure facilities, services, communities) and relationships (depends_on, disrupts, serves, etc.) from reports, news articles, and datasets.

Phase 2: Ontology Design

Define node types (e.g., PowerPlant, Hospital, WaterTreatment, Community) and relationship types. Create a schema diagram showing how entities connect. Document attributes for each entity type.

Phase 3: Knowledge Graph Construction

Implement the knowledge graph using Python (NetworkX for simple graphs, or Neo4j/RDFLib for advanced students). Populate with at least 30 entities and 50 relationships from the disaster case.

Phase 4: Analysis & Visualization

Query the graph to answer questions such as:

  • Which infrastructure nodes are most critical (high centrality)?
  • What are the longest dependency chains?
  • Which communities are most vulnerable to cascading failures?
  • What would happen if a specific facility failed?

Create visualizations and write a 3-4 page analysis report.

Submission Format

Technical Deliverables

  • Python code/Jupyter notebook (.ipynb)
  • Knowledge graph file (GraphML or JSON)
  • Ontology schema diagram (PNG/PDF)
  • Network visualizations

Written Components

  • Analysis report (3-4 pages, PDF)
  • Documentation of ontology design decisions
  • Reflection on limitations and extensions

Why This Assignment Is Innovative

  • Applies emerging AI/knowledge representation concepts to civil infrastructure
  • Bridges qualitative disaster reports with quantitative network modeling
  • Develops systems thinking through hands-on ontology design
  • Provides flexibility: simple graph (NetworkX) or advanced semantic web (Neo4j/RDF)
  • Connects technical modeling with real-world disaster scenarios
  • Prepares students for research using knowledge graphs and LLMs

Lesson Plan

Mapping Community Isolation During Disasters

Duration

75 minutes

Materials

  • Jupyter notebook
  • Sample datasets
  • Projector
  • Instructions handout

Learning Objectives

Students will be able to:

  1. Conduct accessibility analysis using network data
  2. Compute travel time to lifeline facilities
  3. Analyze equity implications of infrastructure disruptions
  4. Reflect on model limitations and real-world applications

Timing Breakdown

0–5 min: Warm-up Discussion

Open with a question: "How might a road closure during a disaster affect different communities differently?" Collect responses on board/chat.

5–15 min: Mini-Lecture

Brief overview of network accessibility concepts, travel-time calculations, and equity considerations. Show a real-world example (e.g., Hurricane Helene road closures).

15–20 min: Setup & Instructions

Distribute notebooks and datasets. Walk through the workflow step-by-step. Answer clarifying questions.

20–50 min: Hands-on Activity

Students work individually or in pairs to complete the accessibility analysis. Instructor circulates to provide support and troubleshoot issues.

50–65 min: Small-Group Discussion

Students share findings in groups of 3-4. Discuss: Which areas were most affected? What populations might be at greatest risk? What are the limitations of this analysis?

65–75 min: Reflection & Exit Slip

Whole-class debrief. Students write a 3-minute exit slip responding to: "What surprised you about this analysis? What would you want to explore further?"

Facilitation Plan

Before class: Prepare and test notebook & datasets. Ensure all software dependencies are met.

During class: Demonstrate workflow using live coding. Circulate during hands-on time. Lead focused discussions that connect technical work to equity themes.

After class: Review exit slips for misconceptions. Post solutions and additional resources for extended learning.

Assessment

Formative assessment through:

  • Observation during hands-on work
  • Quality of small-group discussion contributions
  • Exit slip reflections

Differentiation Strategies

Support for Beginners

  • Scaffolded notebook with step-by-step guidance
  • Pre-loaded code cells for complex operations
  • Partner pairing with more experienced students

Extensions for Advanced Students

  • Modify parameters (e.g., different travel speeds)
  • Add additional facility types to analysis
  • Create interactive visualizations
